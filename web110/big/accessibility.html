Website Accessibility

The web is part of the fabric of our daily lives, but not everyone and everything that accesses the web is created equal. Creating content and accessible websites is the responsibility of the web designer, and is seen as a “basic human right” (WC3).

“The Web is fundamentally designed to work for all people, whatever their hardware, software, language, location, or ability. When the Web meets this goal, it is accessible to people with a diverse range of hearing, movement, sight, and cognitive ability.” (WC3)

When creating a website there are some key factors to consider such as your intended audience as well as all people and devices that could be accessing your site. Developing to the most basic level of need is critical for helping everyone to be more successful while using the web. A screen reader is an example of a tool that is used to make the web more accessible. It is also important to realize that not everyone can use a mouse and that you need to include alternative texts for images, keyboard inputs, and audio transcripts (WC3).

There is a growing industry around web accessibility with companies like AudioEye, UserWay or accessiBe. AudioEye stands out as a leader in this space and is a member of the WC3 (AudioEye). Partnering with the right tools can make accessibility possible for even the most scrappy web designers.
